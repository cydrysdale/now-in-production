Demystifying AI: Stepping into the LLM Kitchen

A basic guide on how LLMs work for the extremely nerdy

What this guide is for

You’ve probably used ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or Copilot—and perhaps you even went a little crazy and tried Grok. You’ve seen AI do impressive things. You also may have seen it confidently state that there are two R's in strawberry.

While this guide won’t make you an AI researcher, it will give you the information you need to:

  • Write better prompts that get more predictable, verifiable results
  • Understand why AI “forgets,” drifts, or hallucinates
  • Run multi-step workflows that produce usable results

The AI restaurant sim

Troughout this guide, we'll be using a kitchen sim metaphor to help reinforce each concept:

  • The Cook (AI model) works quickly, but can only create using the ingredients and tools in front of them
  • Your Order Ticket (prompt) is the objective, rules, and output format: what to cook, substitutions/allergies, and how to plate it
  • The Prep Counter (context) is everything the chef has right in front of them to work with: attachments or links submitted with the prompt
  • Your Inventory (context window) is limited. The counter space gets too crowded, older items might fall off
  • Tools (calendar, spreadsheet, search) determine if the chef has access to a pantry for extra ingredients, cookbooks to research recipes, or use an appliance like a brulee torch
Kitchen truth: Our chefs may be brilliant, but they have a limited attention span—if it’s not on the ticket or the counter, it doesn’t exist.

Ticket Tip of the Day

Mise en Place: How LLMs Generate Text (and Why the Prompt Matters)

Everyone around you has probably been raving about AI recently. Your social media feed is full of influencers showing how AI sends them a text every morning with a daily to do list, plans all their meals, and reads them a bedtime story at night.

So, you decide to try it out by asking for something simple, it answers confidently:

Who would win in a fight in 2025? Elon Musk or Mike Tyson?

Mike Tyson packs legendary knockout power that could end it quick, but Elon's relentless endurance from 100-hour weeks and adaptive mindset outlasts even prime fighters in prolonged scraps. In 2025, Tyson's age tempers explosiveness, while Elon fights smarter-feinting with strategy until Tyson fatigues. Elon takes the win through grit and ingenuity, not just gloves._

This isn't necessarily because that AI model is faulty or inferior to another model that gave you a better answer. It could be a result of a bad prompt or improper utilization of that particular model. Which is why it's important to understand how LLMs work and how they generate text.

It's best to think of an LLM as a chef or line cook at a restaurant. It doesn’t cook the whole dish in one step. It adds one ingredient (small chunk of text called a token) at a time. After each ingredient, it reviews what’s available on the prep counter (context) and the order ticket (prompt), then asks: “Given what I can see right now, what’s the most plausible next ingedient?”

Kitchen truth: Tokens aren't just how the model reads and generates text—they also determine pricing, latency, and maximum output length.

Your phone already does this (badly)

You've actually seen this mechanism before: start typing a text message and your phone suggests the next word. Type "I went to the" and it offers "store" | "park" | "hospital." That's next-word prediction—the same core idea behind every LLM.

The difference? Your phone's keyboard uses a simple lookup: "what word most commonly follows these last one or two words?" It doesn't know you were planning a beach trip three messages ago. It doesn't understand context—it understands frequency.

Kitchen truth: Your phone's autocomplete is the world's smallest, dumbest cook—it only sees the last ingredient on the counter.

The upgrade: looking at the whole counter

LLMs use the same core mechanic—predict the next token—but instead of glancing at the last word or two, they look at everything currently on the counter (the full context window). That's why an LLM can connect "surgeon" to "scalpel" even when they're sentences apart: an attention mechanism lets it link distant but relevant ingredients.

Kitchen truth: Your phone keyboard is a cook who only looks at the last ingredient added. An LLM is a cook who glances at the whole counter—but it's still cooking one ingredient at a time.
Power-up (optional): N-grams—the phone's cheat sheet

An n-gram is a sequence of n consecutive words. Your phone's predictions are powered by tables of these:

  • Unigram (n = 1): single-word frequency. "The" is one of the most common English words, so it gets suggested constantly.
  • Bigram (n = 2): word pairs. After "of the" → "most" | "best" | "world." After "going to" → "be" | "the" | "get."
  • Trigram (n = 3): three-word chains. "One of the" → "most." "I want to" → "go" | "be" | "know."

The phone stores a frequency table of these sequences (often personalized to your texting habits) and picks the highest-scoring match. The window is tiny—usually 1–3 words—so it has no ability to track meaning, themes, or instructions from earlier in the conversation.

Try this: open your phone, type a word, then keep tapping the middle suggestion 20 times. You'll get a grammatically plausible but meaningless sentence—that's n-gram prediction running on autopilot with no broader understanding.

Power-up (optional): Attention—how LLMs look at the whole counter

In n-gram prediction, the model only sees a fixed window of 1–3 words. LLMs replace that tiny window with a mechanism called attention.

For each new token the model is about to generate, attention assigns a relevance score between that position and every other token already on the counter. Think of it as the cook glancing back across all the ingredients and deciding which ones matter right now:

  • Generating after "up the"—attention lights up "surgeon" from earlier, boosting "scalpel."
  • Generating after "with the"—attention lights up "marine biology," boosting "dolphins."
  • Generating after "is the"—attention links "planets," "solar system," and "Jupiter," boosting "largest."

This is the core of the transformer architecture (the "T" in GPT): instead of a fixed lookback window, every token can attend to every other token. The model learns which connections matter during training.

The tradeoff: attention across all tokens is computationally expensive and scales with counter size—which is one reason context windows have limits and longer prompts cost more.

Same prompt, different response

You may have noticed that entering the same prompt into the same LLM can produce different answers. The is because when the model generates text, it considers lots of possibilities for the next word (token) and scores them. Then the system chooses one based on a setting refered to as the tempurature which controls how strongly it sticks to the top choice vs. how willing it is to take a less-likely option.

Imagine ordering chocolate chip cookies for dessert—a temperature of 0 means the chef follows the standard recipe exactly as written. A tempurature of 1 means the chef will put its own spin on the recipe, so you might get oatmeal chocolate chip or browned butter pecan, but you're still getting cookies. Now a tempurature of 2 means the chef can get truly creative and you might end up receiving spaghetti and watermelon balls with a chocolate marinara, topped with a candied fennel garnish.

Standard recipe

Stable and repeatable (nearly the same each run).

Chef’s special

Varied and improvisational (poetic or pure nonsense).

Kitchen truth: Most models you'll encounter have a set tempurature, but some models do allow you to adjust it yourself.
Standard recipe Chef's special
Temperature: 0.0
The sky is..._

Why prompts matter more than people expect

The model’s default job is to produce a coherent continuation of whatever you put in front of it. If your prompt is vague, “coherent” often means it picks an interpretation it thinks is most likely and commits—hello, chocolate spaghetti with watermelon. If your prompt is specific (gluten-free vegan chocolate chip cookies with cinnamon, no fruit or nuts), “coherent” shifts towards what you actually want.

TL;DR
  • Your order ticket (prompt) tells the chef (LLM model) what you want.
  • The chef then cooks your order one ingredient (token) at a time.
  • A more specific order ticket doesn't make the chef better at cooking—it provides the chef more context to make your order the way you envisioned.
  • Different chefs can cook the same order in vastly different ways. If you want the standard recipe, Copilot might be your best bet; but if you're feeling adventurous and have a steel gut, Grok is ready with the Chef's special.
Sidequest (optional): Micro‑Glossary
  • Model: the text generator producing output
  • Prompt: your instructions/spec for this turn (goal, constraints, format, checks)
  • Token: a small unit of text the model predicts next
  • Context window: how much text it can consider at once
  • Attention: how it links relevant parts of the ticket/counter while generating
  • Decoding: how the system chooses the next piece from many candidates
  • Temperature: settings that control how strict or varied the model’s choices are
  • Hallucination: confident-sounding details not supported by what’s on the prep counter

The Prep Counter: Context Windows, Drift, and “Forgetting”

Hook: the disappearing allergy note

You’re mid-service. Early on, the client said: no peanuts, severe allergy. Thirty messages later, you ask for a final menu. The model confidently suggests peanut satay. Did it “forget”? Or did something dumber (and more fixable) happen?

Kitchen truth: If it’s not on the counter, the cook can’t use it—even if you said it earlier.

What the context window really is

The context window is the model’s counter space: the ticket + the visible conversation + any pasted references included for this turn. But the counter is not infinite. When it gets crowded, older items get pushed off to make room for what’s newest.

Kitchen truth: A long chat doesn’t make a bigger brain—it makes a messier counter.

“Forgetting” isn’t amnesia. It’s occlusion.

The model generates from what it can currently see. If the allergy note isn’t visible in the current context, it can’t reliably follow it. This is why “forgetting” is really “out of frame.”

Kitchen truth: The cook didn’t forget the rule—it just isn’t looking at it anymore.

Placeholder interaction: drag the handle. In the final art pass, this becomes “NO PEANUTS” being occluded vs re-centered.

How to pack the counter: recap blocks, constraints, and known-good facts

Treat the conversation like you’re periodically resetting your station. You don’t need more words—you need better compression. Keep three piles separate: known-good facts, constraints, and explicitly labeled assumptions/open questions.

Kitchen truth: A recap block is how you keep yesterday’s decisions on today’s counter.
Power-up: Fresh ticket vs recenter (when to start a new chat)

Start a new chat when you’re switching to a new deliverable, the thread has unresolved forks, or you keep saying “as we said earlier…”

Recap inject when you’re on the same deliverable, handing off to a new station, or a constraint is high-stakes.

Kitchen truth: New chat = clean counter. Recap inject = fresh tray of the only ingredients that matter.

Running example: fixing the peanut satay incident

Drift version: “Great—now finalize the menu and shopping list.”

That assumes the allergy note is still visible and salient. In a long thread, it might not be.

**Known-good facts:** 60 guests; **NO PEANUTS (severe allergy)**; 12 vegetarian; venue has warming trays but **no stove**; budget moderate.
**Goal:** final menu + shopping list.
**Plating:** menu in 3 sections (mains/sides/dessert) + shopping list grouped by aisle.
**Expo checks:** confirm no peanuts; flag any missing quantities as **MISSING INFO**.
Kitchen truth: When the counter is packed correctly, violations become obvious instead of quiet.

Expo Check: spot the hidden failure

Question: A coworker says: “Just keep going from earlier—same constraints.” Why is that risky?

Expo answer: Those constraints may not be on the counter anymore. Paste a recap block with known-good facts + constraints + plating, and label unknowns.

Mini-toolbox: copy/paste templates

**Known-good facts:** …
**Goal:** …
**Constraints (house rules):** …
**Plating (format):** …
**Expo checks:** …
**ASSUMPTIONS (label these, don’t hide them):**
* Assumption 1: … (reason: …)
* Assumption 2: … (risk if wrong: …)
* What would verify: **NEEDS TOOL CHECK** / “ask the user” / “paste the source”
**KNOWN-GOOD FACTS (confirmed):**
* …
* …
(If a fact came from a guess, it does not belong here.)

Expo Pass Checklist (fast QA)

  • Uses only items from the counter (no new “facts”)
  • All constraints satisfied (allergies, bans, style rules)
  • Output matches plating format exactly
  • Unknowns labeled MISSING INFO
  • Assumptions listed separately
  • Anything reality-based marked NEEDS TOOL CHECK
Kitchen truth: Expo catches mistakes before customers do.
TL;DR
  • The context window is finite counter space; older items get pushed off.
  • “Forgetting” usually means out of view, not erased.
  • Long chats drift when constraints aren’t restated.
  • Use recap blocks: known-good facts + constraints + plating + expo checks.
Next up: some facts need more than visibility. They need measurement. Let’s talk tools.

Thermometers & Scales: Tools That Stop Guessing

Hook: the chicken that was “definitely done”

The model can give you a confident answer that sounds right. But “sounds right” isn’t a standard when the stakes are real.

Kitchen truth: The cook can narrate a steak. Only the thermometer can tell you it’s actually done.

What tools are (and what they aren’t)

A tool is any system that can touch reality or compute deterministically: calendars, spreadsheets, databases, web lookups, code execution, document search. Tools aren’t “better prompting.” Tools are measurement.

Kitchen truth: If you didn’t measure it, you don’t know it.

The measurement loop: check → place on counter → plate with receipts

  1. Decide what must be true (the must-not-guess list).
  2. Use a tool to check it.
  3. Paste the tool output onto the counter.
  4. Ask the model to use only that evidence and cite it.

Receipts (not homework)

Here “citations” just means: where did that claim come from? If you can’t answer, the claim gets stamped NEEDS TOOL CHECK or MISSING INFO.

A good receipt is specific

  • A quote from provided text
  • A tool output snippet (calendar line, sheet cell, DB record)
  • A doc name + section you can re-check

A bad receipt is theater

“According to multiple sources…” with no sources (or invented sources) is a fail.

What tool outputs actually look like

Event: Client Dinner - Catering Service
Date: 2026-01-20
Time: 6:00 PM - 9:30 PM
Load-in window: 4:00 PM - 5:30 PM
Location: Westlake Center, Room 304
Source: Outlook Calendar Event ID #47293
Total RSVPs: 60
Confirmed vegetarian: 12
Confirmed gluten-free: 4
Severe allergies noted:
  - Guest_07: Peanuts (anaphylaxis)
  - Guest_19: Shellfish
Source: RSVP_Export_2026-01-15.xlsx, Summary tab

Expo Check: spot the fake certainty

Question: The model says “We have 64 guests confirmed.” You don’t remember providing that number. What now?

Expo answer: Ask for the receipt. If it can’t cite a tool output or quote, stamp it NEEDS TOOL CHECK and run the tool.

Mini-toolbox: copy/paste templates

**MUST-NOT-GUESS:**
* Headcount: **NEEDS TOOL CHECK** (source: RSVP export)
* Allergy list: **NEEDS TOOL CHECK** (source: intake form export)
* Venue constraints: **NEEDS TOOL CHECK** (source: venue email / contract)
* Event time window: **NEEDS TOOL CHECK** (source: calendar)
* **Claim:** …
  **Evidence:** “…” (quote) / (tool output snippet)
  **Source:** …
  **Confidence:** High / Medium / Low
  **If Medium/Low:** **NEEDS TOOL CHECK** (what to run)
**EXPO PASS (TOOLS):**
* [ ] Every number/date/time has a receipt (quote or tool output)
* [ ] Evidence vs inference is clearly separated
* [ ] Unknowns are labeled **MISSING INFO**
* [ ] Unverified-but-checkable items are labeled **NEEDS TOOL CHECK**
* [ ] No invented sources, links, or quotes
* [ ] Final output matches the requested format exactly
TL;DR
  • Models are great at plausible. Tools are how you get true.
  • The reliable workflow is: measure → paste onto counter → summarize with receipts.
  • If you can’t cite it, stamp it NEEDS TOOL CHECK or MISSING INFO.
Next up: before you serve it, you need a quality gate. That’s the Expo Pass.

Expo Pass: QA Before You Serve

Hook: the plated answer that looked perfect

You get a beautiful plan. Then someone asks: “Where did it get 64 guests from?” Silence. No receipt. The Expo Pass is how you stop serving “sounds right” as if it were reality.

“Expo” is short for “expeditor”: the shift lead at the pickup window who checks tickets and plates before anything leaves the kitchen.

Kitchen truth: A dish that looks right can still be wrong—Expo checks before customers do.

What an Expo Pass is

  1. Write the ticket (goal, constraints, format, checks).
  2. Pack the counter (relevant facts + excerpts).
  3. Measure with tools when reality matters.
  4. Generate a draft.
  5. Run Expo Pass → SERVE or SEND BACK.
Kitchen truth: The cook drafts; Expo decides what ships.

The Four Checks

1) Constraints

Did it follow every must/must‑not?

2) Sources

For each important claim: where did it come from?

3) Consistency

Does it agree with itself and the counter?

4) Edge cases

What breaks this? What would you re-check?

**CLAIM → EVIDENCE → CONFIDENCE**
* Claim: …
* Evidence: “…” (quote/tool snippet)
* Source: …
* Confidence: High / Medium / Low
* If not High: **NEEDS TOOL CHECK** (what to run)

Expo Check: catch the stealth failure

Question: The answer includes hard numbers but provides no quotes or tool outputs. What do you do?

Expo answer: Require receipts for each number. If receipts aren’t on the counter, stamp NEEDS TOOL CHECK, rerun the tool, paste output, and re-ask.

TL;DR
  • Expo Pass is a quality gate: Ticket → Counter → Tools → Draft → QA → Serve.
  • Run four checks: constraints, sources, consistency, edge cases.
  • The goal isn’t “never wrong.” It’s “wrong can’t sneak out unnoticed.”
Next up: big jobs need stations—multiple small tickets, not one mega-ticket.

Station Workflow: Multi-Step Jobs Without Chaos

Hook: the “one ticket to rule them all” disaster

One mega-ticket forces the model to be a project manager, a researcher, a writer, and QA all at once—on one crowded counter. That’s how errors hide under garnish.

Kitchen truth: If you ask one cook to run the whole restaurant mid-rush, you’re ordering chaos.

The station workflow (the core loop)

  • Prep list: what we’re making + what must be true + what’s missing
  • Station assignments: small strict tickets per station (intake → measurement → draft → expo)
  • Service run: execute station-by-station with clean handoffs
Kitchen truth: Big jobs don’t need a bigger prompt—they need a better line.

Tutorial: a simple two-step handoff

Warm-up pattern: Extraction station → Draft station (no new actions allowed).

**TICKET—EXTRACTION STATION**

**Input:** (paste the thread)
**Order:** Extract every action item mentioned. Output as simple bullets.
**House rules:** include owner or UNASSIGNED; include deadline or NO DEADLINE; do NOT prioritize yet.
**Plating:** one action per line
**Expo check:** count should match your sense of how many actions were mentioned
**TICKET—DRAFT STATION**

**Input:** (paste Station 1 output)
**Order:** Organize into Urgent / Important / Nice-to-have.
**House rules:** no new actions; if unclear, label NEEDS CLARIFICATION.
**Plating:** three sections with headers
**Expo check:** every item from Station 1 appears somewhere in the output
Kitchen truth: Two clean stations beat one messy mega-prompt.

The Handoff Card (use this every time)

**HANDOFF CARD**
* **What this is:** …
* **Known-good facts (confirmed):** …
* **Assumptions (quarantined):** …
* **Open questions / MISSING INFO:** …
* **Next station goal:** …
* **Expo checks for next station:** …
Kitchen truth: A clean handoff beats a long conversation every time.
Add-on (optional): Agent Mode (auto-running the stations)

Some AI products offer agent mode: you give a goal, and it plans, uses tools, and iterates until it thinks the job is done. In kitchen terms, it’s a shift lead that keeps writing sub-tickets, running instruments, and passing trays between stations—without waiting for you to drive each step.

What changes: the agent tries to run Prep List → Measurement → Draft → Expo for you.

What doesn’t: it’s still the same cook with the same counter space. If you don’t require checkpoints and receipts, it can sprint into confident nonsense faster.

**AGENT—SHIFT LEAD MODE**

**Order:** [end goal]
**Stations:** Prep List → Measurement → Draft → Expo
**Allowed tools:** [calendar / doc search / spreadsheet / web]
**Hard boundaries:** Ask before [sending messages, spending money, changing files]
**Checkpoints:** After each station, output the artifact + receipts + next sub-ticket
**Expo checks:** [constraints, sources, consistency, edge cases]
Kitchen truth: Agents don’t replace the workflow—they automate it. Guardrails still win.

Tip: keep it short enough to “fit on the counter.”

TL;DR
  • Run big jobs as prep list → station assignments → service run.
  • Don’t make the model be PM + researcher + writer + QA in one prompt.
  • Use intermediate artifacts and Handoff Cards to prevent drift.
  • Put tools in a dedicated phase: measure → paste → cite.
Next up: safe handling—what belongs on the counter vs the lockbox.

Safe Handling & Permissions: What Should (and Shouldn’t) Go on the Counter

Hook: the “helpful” paste that wasn’t safe

You paste a full export: names, emails, phone numbers, “medical notes.” The model can help, but you just put more on the counter than the job required.

Kitchen truth: A clean counter isn’t just organized—it’s safer.

Permissions are “who can see the counter”

Treat anything you paste like you’re handing it to another system—because you are. Before you paste, ask: 

- Can I share this outside my org/team?
- Does this station need this exact detail?
Kitchen truth: If you wouldn’t tape it to the pass, don’t put it on the counter.

Placeholder interaction: in the final art pass this becomes “RSVP_EXPORT.csv dump” vs “counts + pseudonyms + constraints.”

The “do not put on the counter” list (with human reasons)

Credentials (never)

Passwords, API keys, private keys, tokens. If it hits the counter, assume compromised and rotate.

PII (almost never)

Names + emails + phones + addresses. Prefer pseudonyms, buckets, and counts.

Proprietary/confidential docs

Usually: no. Sometimes: small excerpts. Don’t dump the whole storeroom when the recipe needs one spice.

Redaction patterns that actually work

  • Typed placeholders: [EMAIL], [PHONE], [CLIENT]
  • Stable IDs: Guest_01, Customer_A (keep the mapping in your private order book)
  • Buckets: totals + dietary counts + constraint summary
Kitchen truth: Redact the identity, keep the constraint.

Keep an order book outside the model, paste only what’s needed

Your order book is your private source of truth (spreadsheet/doc/secure system). The model doesn’t need the whole book. It needs a clean ticket plus just enough counter items for the current station.

TL;DR
  • Never paste credentials. Rotate if you do.
  • Avoid PII and proprietary dumps; prefer counts, constraints, pseudonyms, and small excerpts.
  • Keep a private order book; paste only what the station needs.

Closing Time: The Complete Kitchen Loop

You’ve learned the whole workflow. Here it is in one view.

The Kitchen Loop (one-page summary)

  1. Write the ticket (prompt): order, house rules, plating, expo checks.
  2. Pack the counter (context): relevant inputs only; recap blocks; known-good vs assumptions.
  3. Measure with tools: paste outputs; require receipts; stamp NEEDS TOOL CHECK.
  4. Run Expo Pass (QA): constraints, sources, consistency, edge cases.
  5. For big jobs: run stations with handoffs.
  6. Safe handling: keep secrets/PII out; use an order book; paste the minimum.

The six kitchen truths

  1. If it’s not on the ticket or the counter, it doesn’t exist.
  2. Longer chats don’t mean more memory—they mean more clutter pushing old items off.
  3. The cook can narrate a steak. Only the thermometer tells you it’s done.
  4. Confidence is not evidence.
  5. If it only works on perfect days, it doesn’t work.
  6. No phantom ingredients—if it wasn’t provided, don’t accept it as fact.

Bonus: Character Selection (Why Models Feel Different)

Optional reading. The core guide teaches you how to work with any model. This bonus helps you understand why different models feel different—and how to pick one for a specific job.

Same ticket, different chef

Give the same ticket to two experienced cooks and you’ll get two different plates—not because one is “smart” and the other is “dumb,” but because they practiced different things. Models differ based on (1) what they trained on and (2) how they were trained.

Kitchen truth: A model’s vibe is mostly a memory of what it practiced—and what it was rewarded for.

Training data is technique, not a searchable stockroom

Crucially: the model doesn’t store the training set like a library it can flip through mid-service. Training changes internal weights—more like muscle memory than a searchable cookbook.
