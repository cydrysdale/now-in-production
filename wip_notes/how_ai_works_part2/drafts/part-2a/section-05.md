# Section 5: The Tasting System

## Failure Example

A marketing team delegates their weekly social content calendar to AI. Month one, the output is sharp. Timely topics, varied formats, on-brand voice, a good mix of promotional and thought-leadership posts. The team is impressed. They review each week's calendar, approve it with minor tweaks, and move on.

By month three, something feels off. The topics are safe — nothing edgy, nothing timely, nothing that responds to what's actually happening in the market. The formats have narrowed to the same three templates. The voice has settled into a kind of pleasant corporate hum that sounds like the brand but doesn't sound like anyone in particular. Every individual post is fine. None of them are interesting.

Nobody noticed because each week's calendar was reviewed in isolation. Monday's calendar looked good. So did last Monday's. And the Monday before that. Each plate passed the quality check — but the kitchen was slowly regressing, and the weekly review couldn't see it.

This is the failure mode plating standards alone can't catch. Plating standards tell you whether *this* plate is right. They don't tell you whether the plates are getting worse.

## Theory

Section 4 gave you station assignments — checkpoints along the line so errors get caught before they compound. But those checkpoints are ad-hoc judgment calls: "does this look right?" The cook checks, decides, moves on. For a one-time task, that works. For recurring work — the kind that runs month after month — it's not enough.

The **tasting system** turns those ad-hoc checks into something structured and repeatable. It's the difference between a cook who tastes each dish and a kitchen that tracks what "good" means over time.

### Two kinds of evaluation

Every recurring task needs two different kinds of check, and most teams only do one:

**Quality:** "Is this plate good?"

Hold a single output up against the plating standards and house rules. Does the competitive brief meet the format requirements? Does it follow the sourcing rules? Is the executive summary three sentences or fewer? Quality is a snapshot. It tells you about this plate, right now.

**Regression:** "Is this plate as good as last month's?"

Compare the current output to a known baseline — a plate you know was strong. Are this month's competitive insights as specific as January's? Has the data freshness held, or are the sources aging? Regression is a trend line, not a snapshot. It catches the kitchen that's slowly drifting, one "fine" plate at a time.

For the social content calendar, the quality checks kept passing. Each week's calendar was fine. The regression — the slow drift toward safe, templated, generic content — was invisible because nobody was comparing this month's output to the standard set in month one.

**Regression is often the more dangerous failure.** A bad plate gets caught. A kitchen that's slowly getting worse doesn't — because each individual plate still passes, and "fine" is hard to distinguish from "declining" without a baseline.

### The components of a tasting system

A tasting system for a recurring task has five parts:

**Quality markers.** The specific things you check each time. Not "is this good?" but "is the executive summary three sentences or fewer?" "Does every data point have a source?" "Are all competitors assessed on the same dimensions?" These overlap with plating standards, but they're used differently — plating standards define the spec, quality markers are what you actually check during evaluation.

**Thresholds.** What counts as pass, acceptable, and fail for each marker. "Three sentences or fewer" is pass/fail. "Competitive insights are specific and actionable" needs a defined threshold — otherwise "specific" means whatever the reviewer happens to think today. A threshold like "each competitor entry includes at least one insight the leadership team hasn't seen before" is checkable. "Good insights" is not.

**Known failure modes.** The ways this specific task tends to go wrong over time. For the competitive brief: data going stale because nobody refreshes the sources. Insights becoming generic as the AI converges on safe patterns. The format holding while the content hollows out. Naming the failure modes in advance means you know what to watch for, not just what to hope for.

**Regression checks.** Explicit comparisons to a baseline. Pick a strong output — month one's brief, or whichever edition the team agreed was the standard — and periodically compare the current output against it. Not line by line, but on the dimensions that matter: specificity of insights, freshness of data, actionability of recommendations. If the gap is growing, the kitchen is regressing.

**The walk-away line.** The point at which the task is no longer worth delegating. This is the check most people never define, and it matters more than any individual quality marker. If the competitive brief has regressed to the point where the reviewer is rewriting half of it every month, the delegation has failed — not because the AI can't do it, but because the cost of review exceeds the value of delegation. A clear walk-away line prevents the slow slide from "AI does this well" to "AI does this and I fix it" to "I'm doing this myself but with extra steps."

### Evaluation is part of the input system

The tasting system isn't a postscript. It's part of the specification — as much a design decision as the complete order or the plating standards. You build it before the first plate ships, not after the third month of "something feels off."

Think about the competitive brief. If you wait until month three to define what "specific insights" means, you're reverse-engineering a standard from a plate you've already decided is disappointing. That's rationalizing, not evaluating. But if you define the quality markers upfront — "each competitor entry includes at least one insight the leadership team hasn't seen before" — you discover what "good" actually means to you before the kitchen starts cooking. Often that discovery is the most valuable part: the markers force you to articulate standards you'd otherwise leave implicit.

The regression checks create the other half of the system. Without a baseline, you're evaluating each brief in isolation — and isolated evaluation can't see drift. With one, every month's brief is measured against a known standard. The quality conversation becomes grounded in comparison, not feeling.

## Interactive Module: Spot the Regression

<!-- Interactive module content specification — structured data for implementation -->

Three months of competitive briefs. Each one passed the quality check. But something changed.

### Module Content

**Setup text:** Below are excerpts from three months of the competitive landscape brief — January, February, and March. Each month, the reviewer approved the brief with no major changes. Your job: read all three side by side, then answer the diagnostic questions.

**January (Month 1):**

> *Competitor A's enterprise hiring push (12 new AEs in three regions per LinkedIn data) signals a mid-market-to-enterprise pivot that directly threatens our largest account segment. Their Q4 pricing restructure — dropping per-seat costs while adding platform fees — undercuts our entry pricing by roughly 15% for teams above 50 seats. Recommended action: accelerate the enterprise loyalty program before renewal season.*

**February (Month 2):**

> *Competitor A continues to expand its enterprise sales team and has adjusted its pricing structure to be more competitive in larger deployments. Their recent moves suggest increased focus on the enterprise segment. The competitive pressure on pricing remains notable. Recommended action: monitor the situation and consider adjustments to our enterprise offering.*

**March (Month 3):**

> *Competitor A remains active in the enterprise space with ongoing investments in sales and competitive pricing. They continue to position aggressively in the market. The competitive landscape in the enterprise segment is evolving. Recommended action: continue monitoring competitive developments and evaluate strategic options.*

**Diagnostic questions:**

**Q1: Does each brief pass the plating standards?** (One page, comparison table format, executive summary three sentences or fewer, all claims sourced)

- **Answer:** Yes — all three pass. Format is correct, length is right, structure is intact.

**Q2: Would you catch a problem by reviewing March's brief on its own?**

- **Answer:** Probably not. It's professional, clearly written, meets all the format requirements. If you'd never seen January's brief, March looks fine.

**Q3: Put January and March side by side. What changed?**

- **Answer options (reader selects all that apply):**
  - [ ] The insights got less specific ✓
  - [ ] The data references disappeared ✓
  - [ ] The recommendations became vague ✓
  - [ ] The format degraded
  - [ ] The competitive coverage narrowed
  - [ ] The tone shifted

**Reveal:**

*Visual: January and March side by side with the regressions highlighted — specific data points → vague descriptions, actionable recommendations → "continue monitoring," named competitive moves → generic "remains active."*

January's brief named specific moves, cited data, and recommended concrete action. March's brief described the same landscape in generalities, cited nothing specific, and recommended watching. Every plating standard still passed. The format was intact. The content had hollowed out.

This is regression. Not a bad plate — a kitchen that's slowly losing its edge. The brief didn't fail. It faded. And the only way to see it is to compare this month's plate to one you know was good.

Quality checks ask: *"Is this plate right?"* Regression checks ask: *"Is this plate as good as the one the CMO praised in January?"* The first question keeps passing. The second one catches the drift.

That's why the tasting system needs both.

Look at what you've built across Sections 2 through 5:

- A **complete order** that defines the task without invisible assumptions (Section 2)
- **Plating standards** that define what done looks like, and **house rules** that draw the lines the kitchen can't cross (Section 3)
- **Station assignments** that break the work into checkable pieces with clean handoffs (Section 4)
- A **tasting system** that makes quality visible and catches regression before it becomes invisible (Section 5)

These are the building blocks. Each one is useful on its own — you can write a complete order right now and get better results. Together, they're the raw materials for the higher disciplines: designing the information environment around the task, encoding the priorities that guide judgment calls, and assembling everything into a specification that survives without you hovering over it. That's where Part 2B goes.

> **Webapp chat note:** Even in a simple chat session, you can build a lightweight tasting system. After the AI produces output, check it against two or three specific markers — not "is this good?" but "does every data point have a source?" and "are the insights specific enough to act on?" Save a strong output as your baseline. Next month, compare. You don't need a formal system to start evaluating like one.

---

## Key Lesson

> **Kitchen truth:** Evaluation makes quality legible. Without it, you can't tell the difference between "this is good" and "I've gotten used to this level of output."
