# Section 3: Plating Standards & House Rules

## Failure Example

A marketing analyst delivers a competitive landscape brief that's genuinely impressive. Clean structure, clear comparisons, actionable insights. The data table is tight, the executive summary is scannable, and the analysis surfaces a pattern nobody had noticed — a competitor is quietly hiring enterprise sales reps in three new markets.

The CMO rejects it.

Not because the analysis was wrong. Because the brief speculated about a competitor's product roadmap based on job postings and patent filings. Plausible reasoning — the kind an analyst would be proud of. But company policy requires confirmed public sources only. No inference from hiring patterns. No speculation from patent activity. The legal team made this rule after a competitor cited one of their own speculative briefs in a sales call.

The brief met every quality bar. It was the right length, the right format, addressed the right audience, answered the right question. As a complete order, it was well-executed. But it violated a standing constraint the analyst hadn't written down — one that exists not because the analysis was bad, but because the *consequences* of that kind of analysis had burned the company before.

A complete order defines the task. It doesn't yet define the finish line or the guardrails.

## Theory

Section 2 gave you the complete order — a self-contained task definition that removes the guesses that produce wrong output. But a complete order answers "what needs to be done." It doesn't yet answer two other questions the kitchen needs before a plate leaves the pass:

**"What does done look like?"** — Plating standards.

**"What must never happen?"** — House rules.

These cover different failure modes, and you need both. A plate can meet every plating standard and still violate a house rule. The analyst's brief was well-structured, scannable, and actionable — it met the plating standards. It speculated from non-public sources — it violated a house rule. And a plate can follow every house rule and still miss the standard. A brief that uses only confirmed sources but delivers a rambling five-page narrative instead of a one-page comparison table followed the rules and missed the plate.

### Plating standards: what done looks like

A plating standard is an observable, checkable description of a finished plate. Not a feeling. Not "make it good." Something specific enough that two people reading the same output would agree on whether it passes.

Good plating standards share three properties:

- **Observable.** You can check them without subjective judgment. "The executive summary is three sentences or fewer" is observable. "The executive summary is compelling" is not.
- **Defined before the work begins.** If you define the standard after reading the output, you're rationalizing, not evaluating. The standard must exist before the first plate ships.
- **Specific to this task.** "Professional tone" is generic. "Written for a CMO who skims — no paragraph longer than three sentences, no jargon undefined on first use" is specific to this kitchen.

For the competitive brief, plating standards might include:

- One page maximum, comparison table format
- Executive summary of three sentences or fewer at the top
- Each competitor assessed on the same set of dimensions
- All data points sourced — no unsourced claims in the table

Notice what these are not. They're not aspirational ("insightful analysis"). They're not vague ("high quality"). They're checkable — you can look at the output and say yes or no.

### House rules: the kitchen's standing constraints

House rules are standing constraints that prevent known failure modes. They don't describe the finished plate. They describe the boundaries the kitchen works within, regardless of the specific order.

House rules organize into four quadrants:

| Quadrant | What it means | Example for the competitive brief |
|---|---|---|
| **Must Do** | Non-negotiable requirements | Source every factual claim to a public, dated reference |
| **Must Not Do** | Hard prohibitions | Do not speculate about competitor roadmaps, pricing strategy, or internal plans |
| **Prefer** | Soft defaults that apply unless overridden | Prefer brevity over comprehensiveness when the two conflict |
| **Escalate** | Stop and ask — don't guess | If a competitor makes a major move during the month (acquisition, product launch), flag it rather than folding it into the standard format |

The four quadrants matter because they handle different kinds of risk:

**Must Do** and **Must Not Do** are hard lines. Cross them and the plate gets pulled, no matter how good the rest is. The analyst's brief was rejected not because it scored poorly, but because it crossed a Must Not line.

**Prefer** makes specific, named tradeoffs explicit. "Prefer brevity over comprehensiveness" means the cook doesn't have to decide whether a shorter, sharper brief is acceptable — this particular tradeoff is already resolved. Without Prefer rules, every unnamed tradeoff becomes a coin flip that might not match yours. (Where the broader priorities behind these tradeoffs come from — the standing philosophy that generates them — is a question for later.)

**Escalate** is the quadrant people forget, and it's the one that prevents the most dangerous failures. An Escalate rule says: here is a situation where the cook should stop and ask instead of guessing. A major competitive move mid-month shouldn't be silently absorbed into the standard template. It should trigger a flag. Without an Escalate quadrant, the cook either guesses (risky) or ignores the signal (worse).

Plating standards are specification work — they define what the finished plate looks like. House rules are also specification, but the Prefer and Escalate quadrants touch the edge of something deeper: the standing priorities that shape how the kitchen makes judgment calls. We'll get to that in Section 7. For now, the important thing is that neither system replaces the other. The analyst's brief would have passed any plating standard checklist. What it needed was a house rule that hadn't been posted.

## Interactive Module: Standards & Rules Sorter

### Module Content

**Setup text:** You're building the quality system for the monthly competitive landscape brief. Below are ten requirements. Sort each into one of five bins: plating standards (observable checks on the output) or one of the four house rule quadrants (Must Do, Must Not Do, Prefer, Escalate). Some items feel like they could go either way — that's the point. The boundary between "this defines the plate" and "this constrains the kitchen" is exactly the distinction this section teaches.

**Categories:**

| Category | What it means |
|---|---|
| Plating Standard | An observable check that defines "done" — you can verify it by looking at the output |
| Must Do | A non-negotiable requirement the output must always satisfy |
| Must Not Do | A hard prohibition — crossing this line gets the plate pulled |
| Prefer | A soft default — follow it unless there's a good reason not to |
| Escalate | Stop and flag — don't guess on this one |

**Item 1: "The brief is one page or shorter."**
- **Correct answer:** Plating Standard
- **Feedback:** This is a checkable property of the finished output. You can verify it by looking at the document. It defines what the plate looks like.

**Item 2: "Source every factual claim to a public, dated reference."**
- **Correct answer:** Must Do
- **Feedback:** Non-negotiable. Every claim needs a source, every time. This isn't about what the plate looks like — it's about what the kitchen must always do, regardless of the specific order.

**Item 3: "Do not speculate about competitor internal strategy, roadmaps, or pricing plans."**
- **Correct answer:** Must Not Do
- **Feedback:** Hard prohibition. This is the rule the analyst's brief violated. It exists because the consequences of speculation have burned the company before — not because the analysis was bad, but because the risk isn't worth it.

**Item 4: "Prefer recent quarter data over historical trends when space is limited."**
- **Correct answer:** Prefer
- **Feedback:** This is a judgment call the cook would otherwise have to make silently. "When space is limited" is the key phrase — it's a soft default, not an absolute. If historical trends tell a more important story this month, override the preference.

**Item 5: "The executive summary is three sentences or fewer."**
- **Correct answer:** Plating Standard
- **Feedback:** Observable and checkable. Count the sentences. This defines the plate.

**Item 6: "If a competitor is acquired or makes a major product announcement during the month, flag it for special handling rather than incorporating it into the standard format."**
- **Correct answer:** Escalate
- **Feedback:** This is the Escalate quadrant in action. A major competitive move changes the brief's purpose — the standard template might not be the right container. The cook should stop and ask rather than trying to fold an acquisition into a comparison table.

**Item 7: "Each competitor is assessed on the same set of dimensions."**
- **Correct answer:** Plating Standard
- **Feedback:** Structural check on the output. Are the same columns used for each competitor? You can verify this by looking at the table.

**Item 8: "Include win/loss context for any deal mentioned."**
- **Correct answer:** Must Do
- **Feedback:** This one's arguable. You could read it as a plating standard — it's checkable (does win/loss context appear next to each deal?). But the intent is non-negotiable: a deal reference without win/loss context is incomplete, and this applies every time regardless of format. That makes it a standing requirement the kitchen must always satisfy, not just a property of the plate's appearance. When an item is both checkable *and* non-negotiable, ask which failure is worse — a plate that looks different, or a plate that's missing something the reader needs. That tells you which bin it belongs in.

**Item 9: "Prefer the CMO's strategic priorities as the organizing frame over chronological order."**
- **Correct answer:** Prefer
- **Feedback:** Another judgment call made explicit. Organizing by the CMO's priorities is usually better than organizing by date — but if the CMO hasn't stated priorities this quarter, chronological order is a reasonable fallback. Prefer rules have a built-in override valve.

**Item 10: "If the brief would require using a source the team hasn't previously verified, escalate rather than citing it."**
- **Correct answer:** Escalate
- **Feedback:** Unverified sources are a known risk area. The cook shouldn't decide alone whether a new source is trustworthy — that's a judgment call that belongs to the person who owns the brief's credibility.

**Reveal (after all 10 sorted):**

*Visual: The ten items organized into their five categories, with a count per category.*

Three plating standards. Two Must Do rules. One Must Not Do rule. Two Prefer rules. Two Escalate rules. Together, they form a quality system that covers different kinds of failure:

- Plating standards catch plates that don't look right.
- Must Do / Must Not Do rules catch plates that cross hard lines.
- Prefer rules catch the silent judgment calls that produce inconsistency.
- Escalate rules catch the situations where guessing is worse than asking.

The analyst's brief would have passed every plating standard. It was the missing Must Not rule that sank it. That's why you need both systems — standards alone tell you what done looks like, but not what's off-limits. Rules alone tell you what to avoid, but not what to aim for.

Take the competitive landscape brief and build both halves of the quality system.

**Step 1: Write three plating standards.**

These should be observable checks you can verify by looking at the output. Think: what does a passing plate look like?

**Step 2: Write one constraint in each quadrant.**

| Quadrant | Constraint |
|---|---|
| **Must Do** | *Example: Source every factual claim to a public, dated reference* |
| **Must Not Do** | *(your rule)* |
| **Prefer** | *(your rule)* |
| **Escalate** | *(your rule)* |

**Step 3:** Look at what you've written. Could a colleague use these to judge a brief they'd never seen before? If a plating standard requires insider knowledge to check ("is this insightful?"), it's not observable enough. If a house rule requires judgment the cook doesn't have ("use good sources"), it's not specific enough.

The plating standards define the plate. The house rules define the kitchen. Together with the complete order from Section 2, you now have a task that's defined, has a visible finish line, and has guardrails that prevent known failures.

But all of this assumes the task runs as one unbroken action. What happens when the work is too big for one pass — when the cook needs to run multiple stations and check each one before moving to the next? That's the next building block.

## Key Lesson

> **Kitchen truth:** The quality bar must be visible before the work begins, not only after disappointment.
