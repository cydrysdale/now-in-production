# Section 7: The House Philosophy — Intent Framing

## Failure Example

The competitive landscape brief comes back and it's thorough. Eight pages of analysis — competitive positioning mapped across six dimensions, pricing comparison tables, market share estimates, a trend analysis going back three quarters, and a section on emerging threats that nobody asked for but is, honestly, impressive.

The CMO scans the first page, flips to page two, and closes the document.

She needed a one-pager to skim in the elevator before a board meeting. Something she could glance at, absorb the three things that changed this month, and walk in with a view. What she got was a research paper. Every fact is sourced, every analysis is defensible, the comparisons are structured, nothing speculative, nothing unsourced. The data quality is impeccable. The brief is eight times longer than anyone will read.

The kitchen optimized for depth. The stakeholder needed brevity. The kitchen optimized for completeness. The stakeholder needed signal. The kitchen answered "what can we say about the competitive landscape?" The question was "what does the CMO need to know before she walks into that room?"

Nothing was wrong with the context. The right materials were on the counter. Nothing was wrong with the order — it said "competitive landscape brief for the leadership team." Nothing was wrong with the house rules — confirmed sources only, no speculation, every claim backed up. The brief followed every constraint and violated no prohibitions. But without a standing priority that says *this brief exists for a CMO who reads on her phone in the elevator, not an analyst who reads at her desk* — the kitchen defaulted to more. More analysis, more dimensions, more pages. It optimized for the wrong thing.

## Theory

Section 6 ended with a distinction: context design tells the kitchen what's on the counter. Intent framing tells it what kind of restaurant this is.

That's the gap this section fills. You've built a context packet — fresh, curated, organized by type. But a well-stocked counter doesn't tell the cook whether tonight's plates should be fast and clean or slow and layered. Whether to prioritize the data the leadership team hasn't seen or the data that confirms what they already believe. Whether "competitive landscape brief" means a comprehensive reference document or a decision-support one-pager.

Those are priority decisions. And the dangerous thing about priority decisions is that they feel obvious — to the person who holds them. The CMO knows she wants brevity. She doesn't think of it as a priority decision because to her it isn't one. It's just how the brief should work. But the cook has never been in a board meeting. The cook doesn't know that the CMO reads documents on her phone in the elevator. The cook only knows what's on the counter and what the ticket says. And the ticket said "competitive landscape brief for the leadership team" — which is, to a cook optimizing from first principles, a depth problem.

**Intent framing** is the discipline of making those invisible priority decisions explicit. Not as aspirational language ("we value clarity"). Not as brand tone ("professional but approachable"). As operational standing orders: when two good options compete, here's which one wins and why.

### The house philosophy vs. house rules

Section 3 taught house rules — specific, scoped constraints organized into four quadrants. "Source every claim." "Don't speculate about roadmaps." "Prefer brevity over comprehensiveness when the two conflict." "Escalate major competitor moves."

House rules are useful. They catch known failure modes. But look at that Prefer rule again: *prefer brevity over comprehensiveness when the two conflict.* Where did that rule come from? Someone decided that brevity matters more than depth for this brief. That decision didn't emerge from the task definition or the context packet. It came from a standing philosophy about what this deliverable is for — a philosophy that says this brief exists to support decisions, not to demonstrate analytical rigor.

That standing philosophy is the house philosophy. House rules are the specific policies that follow from it. The philosophy generates the rules; the rules enforce the philosophy.

The distinction matters because you can post house rules without ever articulating the philosophy behind them — and then the next tradeoff the kitchen encounters, the one you didn't write a rule for, gets resolved by a coin flip. The analyst's eight-page brief followed every posted rule. It didn't violate a single Must Not. It just encountered a tradeoff the rules didn't cover — depth vs. brevity at scale — and picked the wrong side because nobody had posted the standing priority.

A house philosophy doesn't replace house rules. It generates them. And it covers the gaps between them.

### What a house philosophy actually contains

A house philosophy answers one question at multiple levels: **when two valid approaches compete, which one wins?**

In a restaurant, this is the difference between a farm-to-table bistro and a high-volume steakhouse. Both serve good food. Both have standards. But their standing priorities are different in ways that shape every plate:

- The bistro: fresh over fancy. If you're out of something, 86 it — never substitute silently. Simplicity is the point.
- The steakhouse: consistency over creativity. The ribeye tastes the same Tuesday and Saturday. Reliability is the point.

Neither is wrong. But a cook who doesn't know which kitchen they're in will make the wrong call on every unmarked tradeoff.

For the competitive brief, the house philosophy resolves the questions the house rules can't reach:

| Priority question | What it resolves |
|---|---|
| **What is the top priority?** | The one thing the brief must always deliver, even if other things slip. For this brief: actionable signal the CMO can use in the next conversation. |
| **What is the second priority?** | The thing that matters most after the first priority is secured. For this brief: current, specific data — not trends or historical patterns unless they explain a live move. |
| **What is the default optimization?** | When no other signal exists, what should the cook optimize for? For this brief: brevity and scannability over depth and comprehensiveness. |
| **What are the non-delegable decisions?** | Judgments the cook should never make alone — not because they're hard, but because the consequences of getting them wrong fall on someone else. For this brief: whether to include or exclude a major competitive development that could change the company's response. |

Notice what these are not. They're not plating standards — those define what the finished plate looks like. They're not house rules — those prevent specific failures. They're the standing priority hierarchy that *generates* both. "Brevity over depth" is the philosophy. "One page maximum" is the plating standard it produces. "Prefer recent quarter data over historical trends when space is limited" is the house rule it generates.

### Why "obvious" priorities are the most dangerous gap

The eight-page brief was a priority failure, not a quality failure. The CMO's preference for brevity felt so obvious to her that she never thought to state it. It's the same reason a farm-to-table chef doesn't post a sign saying "we use fresh ingredients" — of course they do. That's the whole point.

But the cook isn't the chef. The cook doesn't know what's obvious to the person who designed the menu. And when the kitchen is AI, the cook has *no* institutional memory, *no* sense of what the leadership team cares about, and *no* ability to read the room. Every priority that isn't explicitly stated is a priority that doesn't exist.

This is what separates intent framing from the other disciplines. Context design is about what's available. Prompt craft is about what's asked. Spec writing is about what survives handoffs. Intent framing is about what *matters* — the standing judgment calls that shape every output even when the specific task doesn't mention them.

If priorities are only obvious in hindsight, they were never encoded.

## Interactive Module: The Priority Ladder

<!-- Interactive module content specification — structured data for implementation -->

Five tradeoffs the competitive brief encounters. For each one, choose which side wins and write a one-sentence decision rule that makes the tradeoff explicit.

### Module Content

**Setup text:** Every deliverable faces tradeoffs where two good approaches compete. The competitive landscape brief is no different. Below are five pairs of values in tension. For each pair, decide which side wins for this brief and write a one-sentence rule that makes the priority explicit. There are no wrong answers — but there are unstated ones, and unstated priorities are where the eight-page brief came from.

**Mechanic:** Ladder — binary choice cards with text input. For each pair, the reader selects which side wins, then writes a one-sentence decision rule. After all five pairs are completed, reveal shows the before/after comparison.

**Pair 1: Comprehensive vs. Scannable**

> The brief could cover every competitive move in detail, or it could surface only the three to five moves that matter most this month. One gives the reader everything. The other gives the reader signal.

- **Side A:** Comprehensive — include all significant competitive activity so leadership has the full picture
- **Side B:** Scannable — surface only the top moves and keep the brief to one page so leadership actually reads it

*Decision rule prompt:* Write one sentence that resolves this tradeoff. (Example: "When comprehensiveness and scannability conflict, the brief prioritizes the three to five most significant moves — a full log belongs in an appendix, not the main brief.")

**Pair 2: Nuanced vs. Clear**

> Some competitive moves are ambiguous — Bolt's pricing change might be aggressive expansion or defensive repricing. The brief could present both interpretations or pick the most likely one and state it plainly.

- **Side A:** Nuanced — present competing interpretations so leadership can form their own view
- **Side B:** Clear — state the most likely interpretation and flag uncertainty only when it materially changes the recommendation

*Decision rule prompt:* Write one sentence that resolves this tradeoff. (Example: "Default to the clearest reading. If the ambiguity is large enough to change the response, flag it as a contested read — otherwise, commit to a position.")

**Pair 3: Timely vs. Complete**

> The brief is due Monday. On Friday, Cirrus announces an acquisition. You have preliminary data but incomplete analysis. The brief could go out on time with partial coverage, or slip a day for a complete picture.

- **Side A:** Timely — ship on Monday with preliminary coverage and a flag that the analysis is developing
- **Side B:** Complete — hold the brief until the Cirrus acquisition can be properly analyzed, even if it means a one-day delay

*Decision rule prompt:* Write one sentence that resolves this tradeoff. (Example: "Ship on time. A one-paragraph preliminary flag on an emerging move is more useful Monday morning than a complete analysis that arrives Tuesday.")

**Pair 4: Consistent vs. Responsive**

> The brief has a standard format: comparison table, executive summary, three-sentence recommendations. This month, a competitor's acquisition changes the landscape enough that the standard format doesn't capture it well. The brief could stick to the template or restructure.

- **Side A:** Consistent — maintain the standard format so leadership knows what to expect and where to find information
- **Side B:** Responsive — restructure when a major event demands it, so the brief's form matches the significance of the content

*Decision rule prompt:* Write one sentence that resolves this tradeoff. (Example: "Use the standard format unless a move triggers the Escalate rule. If escalated, the format serves the event — not the other way around.")

**Pair 5: Forward-looking vs. Grounded**

> The brief could focus on what competitors did this month (grounded, verifiable) or on what their moves signal about next quarter (forward-looking, more speculative). Both are useful to leadership.

- **Side A:** Forward-looking — leadership wants to know where the market is heading, not just where it's been
- **Side B:** Grounded — stick to confirmed moves and sourced data; let leadership draw their own conclusions about trajectory

*Decision rule prompt:* Write one sentence that resolves this tradeoff. (Example: "Lead with confirmed moves. A forward-looking interpretation is allowed only when grounded in specific, sourced evidence — never from pattern inference alone.")

### Reveal

**Before: No priority ladder stated**

The brief with no explicit priorities is the eight-page brief. The cook encountered all five tradeoffs and resolved each one independently, defaulting to whatever seemed most thorough. Comprehensive won over scannable. Nuanced won over clear. Complete won over timely. The cook's default in the absence of priorities is *more* — more coverage, more hedging, more analysis, more pages. That's not a bad instinct. It's just not the CMO's priority.

**After: Five explicit decision rules**

*Visual: The reader's five decision rules displayed as a priority ladder — most important at the top, flowing down to the most situational.*

Five sentences. That's all it took to resolve the tradeoffs that produced the eight-page brief. Each decision rule names the tradeoff, states which side wins, and gives the cook a standing order that holds across monthly runs. The cook no longer needs to guess whether leadership wants depth or signal. The philosophy is posted.

Now look at what those five rules produce downstream:

- The "comprehensive vs. scannable" rule generates the plating standard: one page maximum.
- The "nuanced vs. clear" rule shapes how the analysis section reads — plain positions, not hedged paragraphs.
- The "timely vs. complete" rule creates an implicit house rule: ship on time, flag developing stories.
- The "consistent vs. responsive" rule connects to the Escalate quadrant from Section 3 — major moves break the template.
- The "grounded vs. forward-looking" rule reinforces the Must Not from Section 3 — no speculation, but now the reader sees *why* that rule exists.

The house philosophy didn't replace the house rules. It showed where they came from.

**Step 5:** Read your house philosophy top to bottom. Now imagine a new analyst — someone who has never seen the brief before — reading these four statements. Would they produce a one-pager or an eight-page analysis? Would they optimize for signal or for coverage? If your philosophy doesn't resolve those questions before the kitchen starts cooking, it isn't specific enough yet.

The house philosophy joins the context packet as part of the raw materials for the recipe card. The context packet tells the kitchen what's on the counter. The house philosophy tells it what matters. In Section 8, we'll pull everything together — complete order, plating standards, house rules, station assignments, tasting system, context packet, and house philosophy — into a single specification that can survive without you hovering. That's the recipe card.

> **Webapp chat note:** You can paste your house philosophy directly into a chat session as a set of standing instructions. Try putting the four components (top priority, second priority, default optimization, non-delegable decisions) at the top of your next competitive brief prompt, above the context materials. Compare the output to what you got without them. Most people find the difference is not in the accuracy of the facts — it's in which facts the brief leads with and how much space it gives each one.
