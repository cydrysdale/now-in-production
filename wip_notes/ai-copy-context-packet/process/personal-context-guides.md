# Personal Context Guides — Draft

> **Status: Draft exploration.** Part of an active workflow exploration being refined through end of March 2026.

## What This Is

A personal context document captures the knowledge, preferences, and standards that you carry in your head—the things that shape how you evaluate AI output but that the AI has no way of knowing unless you tell it.

When you load organizational context like brand rules and decision rules into an AI session, you are giving the AI the company's perspective. When you also load your personal context document, you are giving it yours. The combination produces output that is closer to what you would have written yourself and easier to evaluate against your actual standards.

## Why This Matters

AI fills gaps with confident-sounding guesses. If you do not tell it what your standards are, what you know about the organization, or how you prefer to work, it will substitute its own defaults. Those defaults are often plausible enough to accept but subtly wrong—the kind of output that looks good but requires rewriting once someone with the right context actually reads it.

A personal context document reduces that gap. It takes about 30 minutes to build the first version and 10 minutes to update each month.

## How To Build One

Answer the questions for your role below. Write in plain language. This is not a formal document—it is a reference file that gets loaded alongside the packet context at the start of AI sessions.

Keep it under 500 words. Every line should earn its place. If removing a line would not change the AI's output, cut it.

This is a working tool, not a performance document. It exists to make AI output better, not to evaluate people.

## Copywriter Context Document

Answer these questions:

### What You Do

- What is your role and what do you produce in a typical week?
- Who are your primary audiences—who reads or uses your output?

### Your Standards

- Think of the best campaign copy you have produced recently. What specifically made it good?
- Think of AI output that disappointed you. What specifically was wrong—not "it was bad," but what qualities were missing or off?

### Institutional Knowledge

- What do you know about the organization, the audience, or the product that a new hire would take months to absorb?
- Are there specific terms, acronyms, or internal concepts that have special meaning in your context?

### How You Work With AI

- What types of tasks do you most frequently use AI for?
- What have you found consistently works well? What consistently fails?
- What format do you prefer output in?

### Decision Rules

- When speed and quality conflict, where is your line?
- What does "good enough" mean for routine work versus high-stakes work?

### Template

```
=== PERSONAL CONTEXT: COPYWRITER ===
Last updated: [date]

ROLE
[What you do, what you produce, who it is for]

CURRENT PRIORITIES
[This quarter's focus areas]

QUALITY STANDARDS
[What makes output good in your specific context—concrete, not vague]

INSTITUTIONAL KNOWLEDGE
[The unwritten rules, special terms, and context that an outsider would not know]

AI INTERACTION NOTES
[What works, what does not, preferred formats]

WHEN IN DOUBT
[3-5 short rules that capture your judgment—e.g., "When unsure whether a claim is approved, leave it out and flag it rather than including it with a qualifier"]
```

## Copyeditor Context Document

Answer these questions:

### What You Do

- What is your editorial role and what types of content do you review?
- What is the quality bar you hold output to?

### Your Standards

- What are the most common issues you fix in AI-generated copy?
- What does "publishable with light edits" actually mean to you—what level of polish crosses the threshold?
- What patterns do you see that look polished but are editorially wrong?

### Known Failure Modes

- What are the recurring editorial problems in your team's AI output?
- What types of errors does AI make that a human writer typically would not?

### How You Work

- What is your preferred editorial workflow—do you edit in-document, use comments, return to the writer?
- What information do you need alongside a draft to review it efficiently?

### Template

```
=== PERSONAL CONTEXT: COPYEDITOR ===
Last updated: [date]

ROLE
[What you review, what your quality bar is]

EDITORIAL STANDARDS
[Specific, concrete—not "clear and concise" but what that means in practice]

COMMON AI FAILURE MODES
[Patterns you fix repeatedly—these should eventually become packet updates]

POLISHED BUT WRONG
[What specifically looks good but is editorially off in your context]

WORKFLOW PREFERENCES
[How you like to receive and return work]

WHEN IN DOUBT
[3-5 short rules—e.g., "If the copy reads like it could have been written for any B2B finance brand, it needs another pass"]
```

## Campaign Manager Context Document

Answer these questions:

### What You Do

- What campaigns do you typically manage? What scope?
- What does success look like for your campaigns?

### Your Standards

- What makes a campaign feel strategically right to you?
- What makes a campaign feel wrong even when the copy is polished?

### Institutional Knowledge

- What do you know about your product, audience, or market that the team relies on you for?
- What strategic context would a new campaign manager need months to absorb?

### Template

```
=== PERSONAL CONTEXT: CAMPAIGN MANAGER ===
Last updated: [date]

ROLE
[What you manage, what success looks like]

STRATEGIC STANDARDS
[What makes a campaign strategically sound in your context]

INSTITUTIONAL KNOWLEDGE
[Product, market, and audience context that others rely on you for]

KNOWN FAILURE MODES
[What goes wrong when campaigns miss—the strategic version of "polished but wrong"]

WHEN IN DOUBT
[3-5 short rules—e.g., "If the campaign spine could apply to any fintech product, the positioning is not specific enough"]
```

## Production Context Document

Answer these questions:

### What You Do

- What parts of the packet system or workflow do you own?
- What handoffs, approvals, or publishing steps do you manage?

### Your Standards

- What does a packet need to include before you consider it runnable?
- What process failures create the most downstream rework?

### System Knowledge

- What versioning, file hygiene, or archival rules matter in your setup?
- What do people routinely forget that causes avoidable QA or publishing problems?

### How You Work With AI

- What AI tasks are useful in your role?
- What should AI never be trusted to infer without explicit human confirmation?

### Template

```
=== PERSONAL CONTEXT: PRODUCTION ===
Last updated: [date]

ROLE
[What parts of the workflow and system you own]

RUNNABILITY STANDARD
[What must be true before a packet should move forward]

PROCESS RISKS
[The recurring workflow, QA, approval, and publishing failures you watch for]

SYSTEM KNOWLEDGE
[Versioning, file structure, naming, archive, and governance context]

AI INTERACTION NOTES
[What AI can help with in your role and what still requires explicit human checks]

WHEN IN DOUBT
[3-5 short rules—e.g., "If approval status is unclear, stop the workflow and mark the gap rather than assuming signoff happened"]
```

## How To Use Your Personal Context Document

1. Build your first version by answering the questions for your role. Aim for 300-500 words.
2. Save it as a reusable file you can access at the start of any AI session.
3. Load it into AI sessions alongside the organizational context (brand rules, decision rules, campaign spec).
4. Update it monthly or when your priorities shift.
5. After each campaign, ask yourself: did the AI miss something I know but did not include? If yes, add it.

## What Not To Include

- Information already in the brand rules, decision rules, or campaign spec. Do not duplicate organizational context.
- Aspirational language or self-descriptions. This is a functional document, not a bio.
- Long explanations. If a line does not change how the AI behaves, cut it.
